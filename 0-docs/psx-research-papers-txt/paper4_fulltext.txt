Executive Summary 
 
 
On a worldwide scale value-at-risk, or VaR, is a widely accepted methodology for evaluating 
risk. Financial institutions (FIs) in Pakistan employ diverse approaches to value at risk (VaR) 
as a result of regulatory requirements. On the contrary hand, risk takers rarely employ VaR in 
their decision-making processes; instead, risk controllers are the ones who measure and report 
VaR. In order to address this gap, this study examines several VaR strategies using a portfolio 
of equity assets listed on the PSX. 
From January 2019 to December 2023, we acquired daily data for the KSE 30 index, and we 
created a hypothetical portfolio of PKR 1 billion. According to the guidelines employed by 
portfolio managers, the portfolio was managed by adjusting investment positions whenever a 
security's market value dropped below 10% of the initial investment cost. For making 
decisions, the much-lauded Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio was employed. 
Various GARCH models were employed to estimate the portfolio's volatility; the most 
appropriate approaches were GARCH (2 , 3 ) and GARCH (1 , 2). Following that, VaR was 
calculated using the variance -covariance method, which considers conditional 
heteroscedasticity in place of static variance t o account for dynamic variance. Compared to 
VaR calculated with static variance, our results show that VaR estimated with dynamic 
variance is more useful in assessing risk since it is significantly more in comparison to real 
historical losses and provides portfolio managers with more action points.

1.1 Overview of the Equity Market 
Equity market, often known as the stock market, works as a key platform where, selling and 
purchasing of shares of publicly traded companies take place. This market plays a vital role  by 
authorize firms to raise capital through the issuance of shares, Besides that it also allow 
investors to be a part of this company and earn profit based on their overall performance. 
Share is considered as core of the equity market. A share denotes a possession in a company, 
reflects a claim of the company's assets and earnings.  
Stock exchanges use as a medium in equity transactions, which are organized and reg ulated 
marketplaces where securities are traded. PSX is a prominent examples of stock exchange. 
Companies have to go through the process of Initial Public Offering (IPO), it is a process that 
permits a private firm to sell their shares publicly. 
There is a  vast list of participants included in the equity market, which includes retail 
investors—individuals who sell and purchase  shares for personal use —and it also includes 
institutional investors, named as convention and Islamic banks, mutual funds, pension funds, 
and insurance companies, typically considered as the large investors in equity market There 
are KSE 30 and KSE 100 indices represents the performance of top 30 and top 100 companies, 
which provide insights into the overall market or specific sectors.. 
Usually shares are categorized as common shares and preferred shares. Common shares offers 
shareholders with voting rights and the power to receive dividends. Preferred shares, normally 
do not carry voting rights but provide a higher claim on assets and  profits, with dividends 
usually being paid out before common stakeholders. 
There are multiple factors that affect share prices, which includes company performance, 
economic indicators, market sentiment, and the basic principles of supply and demand. 
Company performance encompasses financial health, earnings reports, and growth prospects, 
while economic indicators include factors like inflation rates, interest rates, and overall 
economic growth. Market sentiment shows the behavior and perception of stakehol ders, 
influenced by news, trends, and geopolitical events. The interplay of supply and demand also

plays an important role in determining share prices, with the number of shares available for 
sale versus the number of investors willing to buy impacting the market value. 
We can say, the equity market is a dynamic and complex system that helps to raise capital for 
companies and provide investment opportunities for individuals and institutions.  
 
1.2 Overview of Risk Management in Terms of Pakistan Stock Exchange (PSX) 
Risk Management in volves identifying, assessing, and prioritizing risks followed by 
coordinated efforts to minimize, monitor, and control the probability or impact of unfortunate 
events. Effective risk management helps investors safe their investments from uncertain events 
and maximize profits in a volatile market. 
There are multiple risk involves in the stock market such as market risk, liquidity risk, 
operational risk, regulatory risk, and foreign exchange risk. Market risk involves interest rat e 
risk, credit risk and liquidity risk. Borrowing cost can be increased due to rise in increase 
interest rate. If a company is enlisted in PSX, and their shares are traded publicly, fails to meet 
its financial obligations, will impact on stock prices and investor returns, that particular risk is 
named as credit risk. Liquidity risk refers to the inability to sell or purchase a stock within a 
particular period of time without significantly affecting its price . Regulatory risk involves the 
risk that changes i n laws and regulations affect the business, sector or market. Foreign 
exchange risk refers to risk of loss occurred due to change in exchange rate between currencies. 
Investors use to various techniques to mitigate these risks. Investors usually diversify their 
portfolio through different sectors and asset classes to minimize the effect of a below average 
performance of any specific asset class. Hedging techniques are also used to mitigate the effect 
of risk, it includes future, options and other derivative  techniques. Regular monitoring of the 
market condition and accessing their trends helps to makes the possible changes in the 
portfolio. 
 
1.3 Overview of Value at Risk (VaR) and Its Methodologies 
Value at Risk (VaR) is a vital risk management tool that measures the potential loss that can 
be occurred in a specific period of time in portfolio. It shows that  it gives exact monetary 
amount of loss on the portfolio  that can be occurs due to market vola tility under normal 
conditions. Calculation of VaR is really important for financial institutions, portfolio managers, 

   
Page 13 of 50 
 
and individual investors to estimate and manage the risk linked with their portfolio, which helps 
investor to access situation and make proper decisions regarding their investment decisions. 
VaR includes multiple important factors, including the time horizon, confidence level, and loss 
amount. The time horizon reflects the time period over which the risk is estimated, it usually 
estimated from 1 day to several months. The confidence level shows the chances that value of 
VaR will be accurate up to certain percent, wid ely used confidence levels are 95%, 90%, and 
99%.  
VaR is widely calculated using three different methodologies i.e. historical  simulation, 
variance-covariance, and Monte Carlo simulation. In historical simulation method historical 
market data is commonly used to estimate potential losses assuming future performance will 
be based on past performance. First step is to collect histo rical data of returns, then portfolio 
returns are calculated for each period, sorting the returns from lowest to highest, and then at 
last estimating the VaR at a given confidence level. 
 The variance-covariance method based on the assumption that returns are normally distributed, 
and it uses mean and standard deviation to evaluate the VaR. First step is to collect historical 
data of returns, then portfolio returns are calculated for each period,, determining the potential 
loss at the given confidence level  using the properties of the normal distribution, and 
calculating VaR using the formula: VaR = Z * σ * √t, where Z is the Z-score corresponding to 
the confidence level, σ is the standard deviation, and t is the time horizon. 
In Monte Carlo simulation, larg e number of simulations of future prices are generated using 
computer, creates hypothetical scenarios for assets prices based on their statistical properties. 
The process begins with specifying key statistics such as (mean, standard deviation, 
correlations) of the asset returns, Multiple scenarios are used to estimate VaR value, in this 
way various scenarios and their VaR is predicted which helps investors to understand the 
dynamic market conditions and taking informed decisions.  
VaR offers several benefits, including providing a clear and concise value of risk which is easy 
to understand and interpret. This tool is being applicable across different asset classes and 
portfolios. Although there are some limits of VaR. It ass umes ordinary market conditions , 
Values will be underestimating if market dynamics goes through any stressed or unexpected 
situation. Another side effect is that VaR estimated through historical simulation depends on 

   
Page 14 of 50 
 
the previous data, assuming that future values will be based on past even ts and the market 
conditions will remains unchanged. VaR estimated through Variance Co -Variance method 
assumes that data is normally distributed. 
1.4 Overview of Volatility 
Volatility is a statistical measure that assesses the extent of fluctuations in the value of a 
financial asset or market index over a specific period. It is typically calculated using standard 
deviation or variance of returns. Volatility indicates the level of uncertainty and risk associated 
with an asset's price movements, with higher volatility signifying greater price swings and 
lower volatility indicating more stable prices. Various factors contribute to market volatility, 
including economic indicators, geopolitical events, interest rate changes, and shifts in investor 
sentiment. Understanding volatility is vital for investors, as it impacts option pricing, risk 
management strategies, and overall portfolio performance. The Volatility Index  is a widely 
used tool to measure and predict market volatility, providing valuable insights for investors to 
make informed decisions and navigate market fluctuations effectively.

LITERATURE REVIEW 
 
Lim and Sek (2013) compared the performance of GARCH type models for capturing volatility 
in Malaysian Stock Market. They collected data for Malaysian market ranging from Jan 1990 
to Dec 2010. The Malaysian market faced crisis during 1997. Therefore, data collected is 
divided into three parts, i.e., pre-crisis, during crisis and post crisis. The researched concluded 
that for asymmetric and symmetric GARCH models, both models perform differently in 
different time frames. During crisis, asymmetric GARCH perfo rmed well, whereas during 
normal period the results from symmetric GARCH were preferred. The parameters affecting 
volatility were exchange rates and crude oil prices, but theses parameters were insignificant 
during crisis. 
 
Angelidisa, Benosa, and Degiannakis (2004) uses extended forms of ARCH Models for daily 
VaR calculations in a diversified portfolio comprising of five stock indices, having no. of 
sample sizes and distributions.  The main findings concluded that firstly, leptokurtic 
distributions (meaning that they have many outliers), gained ground on calculated VaR 
forecasting. Secondly, having accurate choice of sample size is a must for VaR accuracy. And 
at last, they concluded that the ARCH model having forecasting of VaR with precision is  
different for every portfolio. 
 
Muhammad Asif and Abdul Aziz (2016), investigates the efficiency of GARCH type models 
for predicting stocks return dynamic and volatility for PSX. This case study is closely related 
to our investigation as it focuses on the valuation of share price. Their study helps the capital 
analysts to identify the factor affecting the share price dynamics. They worked on a daily time 
series data from KSE-100 listed in PSX dated from January’1 st 2008 to December’31st, 2015. 
They worked with ARCH f amily model to estimate the cluster volatility. The main finding 
from the study indicates that GARCH (1, 1) is the most appropriate model, indicating that the 
PSX market is weak -form of efficiency (meaning that the future prices are random a nd not 
influenced by the past).

Conceptual Framework of Time Series 
 
3.1 Analysis and the Concept 
Time series analysis is an important aspect for statistical methodology when dealing with data 
recorded at a consecutive time (in even intervals). Whereas, other data types of time series data 
that are sorted in chronological manner. Due to this ordering, we can observe an essential 
pattern including trends cycles, and seasonality. These are important for better understanding 
and predicting the future behavior for the provided data. Trend signifies a sustained change in 
the series, either upward trending, downward trending or constant, reflecting the overall shift 
in which data is moving. Cyclic patterns, transpires at irregular duration impacted by t he 
economical and environmental factors. These irregular durations signify the arbitrary 
deviation, not showing any pattern or trend and are volatile. Seasonality, unlike cyclic, pertains 
to periodic variations transpiring at regular durations, including monthly, quarterly, caused by 
seasonal aspect. 
The time series analysis implementation extends along multiple fields, involving economics, 
finance, engineering and environment science. Let’s consider, in economics, it’s utilized for 
predicting the GDP, unem ployment rates, inflations rates, etc. For the field of finance, time 
series analysis helps in examining the stock prices, interest rates, exchange rates etc. In 
environmental science, time series is used to study about climate changes, water levels etc., 
whereas in engineering, it monitors and analyzes the system performance.   
Stationarity is a core idea in time series analysis, plays a vital role in modelling and forecasting. 
It portrays a series with statistical concepts, for instance mean, variance, au tocorrelation, etc.  
Stationarity is a fundamental concept in time series analysis, crucial for accurate modeling and 
forecasting. It describes a series whose statistical properties, such as mean, variance, and 
autocorrelation, stay unchanged overtime. There are three types of stationarity: 
Strict (or strong) stationarity refers to collective distribution of any set of data points that 
remains consistent regardless of time shifts. It's the most severe form of stationarity. 

   
Page 19 of 50 
 
Weak (or second-order) stationarity refers to the mean of a data not changing over time neither 
its variance. It’s less complex than strict stationarity but vital for various time series models. 
Trend stationarity implies that a series can be converted to stationary by eradicati ng a fixed 
trend component. It concedes that while the series itself may not be stationary, extracting a 
predictable trend can render it. 
Foremost element for the selection of relevant modelling approach and assuring predictable 
predictions to comprehend which type of stationarity is applicable on the provided time series 
data, leading to testing for stationarity, which is an essential step in time series analysis.

3.2 Time Series Models 
Several models are implemented in time series analysis to comprehend and forecast future 
values based on earlier observed data. Expected value models, Moving Averages (MA), 
Autoregressive Moving Averages (ARMA), and Autoregressive Integrated Moving Averages 
(ARIMA), comprise a few of those models. 
3.2.1 Expected Value Models 
An essential concept in statistical and probability theor y is that the expected value, also 
additionally referred to as the mean, is the average or median value of an uncertain variable. 
The expected value in time series analysis assists to determine the central trend that the data 
moves around. Expected value m odels presume that, after incorporating evident trends and 
shifts the series’ predicted future values will likely fall within a range around the historical 
mean. 
3.2.2 Auto Regressive (AR) Models 
AR models utilize a linear combination of past trends in order to predict future values. The AR 
(p) model, where "p" denotes the number of lagged observations, can be represented as:

For the purpose to adequately represent both momentum and shock aspects in the data set,

CONCLUSION 
 
Value-at-Risk is a widely acclaimed method for measuring the risk worldwide. Due to a 
number of regulatory obligations, various approaches of VaR has been practiced by FIs 
operating in Pakistan. So far, the VaR is mostly measured and reported by the risk controllers 
in FIs, whereas, the risk takers by and large, are not using VaR as a tool for decision making. 
In order to utilize this risk metric for decision making purposes various approaches of VaR are 
yet to be explored in the context of Pakistani financial markets which are already applied in 
developed financial markets. 
This study is an attempt to fill this gap, where we explored an alternative approach of VaR on 
a portfolio comprising equity securities enlisted in PSX. For this purpose, we have collected 
the daily data for the constituents of KSE 30 index from January 2019 to December 2023. With 
these securities, we have constr ucted a PKR 1 Bn hypothetical portfolio. While constructing 
the hypothetical portfolio, we tried to mirror the considerations of portfolio managers. For 
instance, if the market value of any security falls below 10% of the original cost of investment, 
it may prompt equity managers to revise their investment position, by means of either selling 
the security or acquire more shares of it for reducing the cost of investment.  
We based the criterion chooses for bifurcating the above underlined binary by means of widely 
celebrated Price-to-Earning (P/E) ratio. Once the portfolio mix is constructed, we estimated 
it’s volatility by means of various GARCH type models as proposed by Bollerslev (1986). 
Amongst numerous applied models, we found GARCH (2, 3) and GARCH (1, 2) are found to 
be most appropriate for capturing the volatility of this hypothetically constructed portfolio. In 
the later stage, we estimated VaR by means of variance -covariance approach for the 
components of our portfolio on standalone basis. 
While going for the application of VaR estimation via variance -covariance approach we used 
the conditional heteroskedastic approach for accounting of the variance factor. Thus, we 
estimated the dynamic variance, instead of conventionally deployed static variance approaches. 
Finally, we have compared the VaR estimation came from dynamic approach with the static 
approach. Our findings proven that VaR estimated through dynamic variance is a more 
effective tool for quantifying risk as compared to the VaR estimated through keeping variance 
constant.  The prime qualifiers of the previous statements are (a) it is more closer to the actual 
losses which were historically incurred and (b) it creates more breaches for the VaR limit, 
which implies more action points for the portfolio managers.
